GENERAL INSTRUCTIONS

1. The BALLOONSAT BASKET comprises 53 project topics (45 subsystem projects and 8 ground segment projects) identified for the design, development, testing, launch and recovery of a high-altitude BalloonSat mission reaching 25–33 km altitude, together with a complete student-built ground station.

2. Projects are organised by subsystem work package — Mission Analysis (MSN), Avionics/OBC/EPS (AVI), Structure & Thermal (STR), Mechanisms (MEC), Data Handling (DHA), Science Payloads (PLD/PLC), Software (SWS), AI/ML (AIM) and Ground Segment (GND) — not by academic branch. Students from any discipline may select any project matching their interest and skills.

3. Each project entry comprises a brief write-up (Summary), Scope of the Work, Expected Deliverables, difficulty grade, recommended team size and linkages to other projects of the mission, to help teams select and prepare their work plans.

4. The recommended team size is 2–4 students per project. Each team shall nominate a team lead who attends the weekly all-hands integration meeting.

5. The programme duration is one semester (5 months). Review gates: Mission Requirements Review (Month 1), Preliminary Design Review (Month 2), Critical Design Review (Month 3), Flight Readiness Review (Month 4), Launch + Post-Flight Review and Final Presentations (Month 5). The detailed plan is given in the Mission Timeline section.

6. Mission-level design constraints binding on all teams: total payload mass ≤ 2 kg; electronics shall survive −60 °C ambient; a single shared battery bus is preferred and every subsystem shall submit a power budget; telemetry shall use licence-free/amateur bands (e.g. 433 MHz LoRa) within regulatory limits; descent rate at ground level shall be 4–6 m/s; aviation authority clearance (NOTAM) is mandatory before launch.

7. Mission success criteria — Minimum: payload launched, partial telemetry received, payload recovered. Full: continuous telemetry, all sensors logged, live tracking on the mission dashboard, payload recovered intact. Stretch: live images downlinked, all science experiments return usable data, AI/ML models validated on real flight data, results published.

8. Every flying item shall pass environmental acceptance testing (cold soak, drop, vibration, burn-in) on the BSAT-STR-2026-004 test rigs before the Flight Readiness Review.

9. Interface control: pinouts, connectors and the telemetry packet format are controlled documents owned by BSAT-AVI-2026-001 and BSAT-SWS-2026-004; changes are frozen at the Critical Design Review.

Title of the Project

Flight Trajectory Prediction & Simulation

Area of Work / Key Skills & Tools

MATLAB/Python, atmosphere models (ISA), CUSF/habhub-style predictors, GFS wind data

Difficulty | Recommended Team Size

Medium | 3 students

Summary of the Project

A latex weather balloon does not fly a straight line — it drifts with the wind at every layer of the atmosphere, climbing at roughly 5 m/s until it expands from ~2 m to ~10 m diameter and bursts near 30 km. Predicting this path is a classic aerospace problem combining buoyancy, drag, the standard atmosphere and real forecast wind fields (GFS). Your prediction decides the launch site, the recovery plan and whether the mission is even legal to fly that day (it must not drift into airports or restricted zones). The core challenge is that ascent rate and burst altitude are uncertain, so a single predicted path is never enough — you must understand how errors grow. Start by studying the physics used by the open-source CUSF flight predictor, then build your own simplified model and compare.

Scope of the Work:

Model the complete flight: ascent rate vs free lift, burst altitude prediction, and descent under parachute. Use GFS wind data to predict the flight path and landing zone. Validate the model against the actual flight and publish prediction-vs-actual error analysis.

Expected Deliverables:

Trajectory simulation tool

Pre-launch prediction report

Post-flight validation report

Title of the Project

Parachute & Recovery System Design

Area of Work / Key Skills & Tools

Aerodynamics, drag estimation, sewing/fabrication, drop-test experimentation

Difficulty | Recommended Team Size

Medium | 3 students

Summary of the Project

After burst, the payload free-falls in air so thin (1% of sea-level density at 30 km) that the parachute barely works at first — descent starts at 50+ m/s and slows as the air thickens. Your parachute must deliver a safe 4–6 m/s at ground level: too fast destroys the payload, too slow drifts it tens of kilometres further, often into unreachable terrain. This is real aerospace decelerator design: canopy shape selection (hemispherical, cross, toroidal), drag coefficient estimation, and the awkward fact that the parachute must also not tangle with the burst balloon remains above it. The engineering happens in testing — drop tests are your wind tunnel. Start with the drag equation, size a canopy for your payload mass, and design a test campaign that measures real descent rates.

Scope of the Work:

Design, size and fabricate the parachute for a 4–6 m/s landing descent rate. Analyze drag coefficients, perform drop tests from a building/drone, and design the rigging between balloon, parachute and payload train.

Expected Deliverables:

Flight-ready parachute + rigging

Drop test report

Descent rate analysis

Title of the Project

Balloon Sizing, Lift Gas & Burst Analysis

Area of Work / Key Skills & Tools

Buoyancy physics, gas laws, safety procedures, trade studies

Difficulty | Recommended Team Size

Beginner | 2 students

Summary of the Project

Everything about the flight is set at the moment of inflation: how much gas you put in the balloon determines ascent rate, burst altitude and flight duration. Too little lift and the balloon may float for hours and land in another state; too much and it bursts low, wasting the mission. The physics is Archimedes' principle applied to a stretching rubber envelope — free lift, neck lift, gas density (helium vs hydrogen trade-offs including safety), and the manufacturer's burst diameter data. On launch day, your team is the one holding the balloon, so the deliverable is as much procedure as physics: a repeatable way to measure neck lift in the field with wind blowing. Start with the balloon performance calculators used by the HAB community and re-derive their math yourself.

Scope of the Work:

Select balloon size (e.g. 800–1500 g latex), compute required neck lift and gas volume (helium/hydrogen trade study), predict burst altitude, and write the complete launch-day inflation procedure with a lift measurement rig.

Expected Deliverables:

Balloon selection report

Inflation & neck-lift procedure

Launch-day checklist

Burst altitude prediction

Title of the Project

Payload Train Dynamics & Stability

Area of Work / Key Skills & Tools

Rigid body dynamics, IMU data analysis, mechanical design

Difficulty | Recommended Team Size

Advanced | 3 students

Summary of the Project

Video from most amateur balloon flights is dizzying — the payload spins and swings like a pendulum under a 30–90 m line to the balloon. Understanding why is a genuine dynamics problem: the payload train is a multi-body pendulum excited by wind shear and vortex shedding from the balloon. Reducing the motion matters for camera footage, for the stabilized gimbal team (BSAT-MEC-2026-002), and for scientific sensors that assume a known orientation. Countermeasures include swivels, fins, longer/shorter lines, and mass distribution changes — but you must model and measure before you fix. The IMU flying on the payload gives you real rotation-rate data to validate against. Start by modeling the train as a double pendulum and identifying its natural frequencies.

Scope of the Work:

Study and reduce spin/pendulum motion of the payload during ascent so cameras stay stable. Design the payload train layout (balloon–parachute–radar reflector–payload spacing), swivels and anti-rotation measures. Validate with IMU data from the flight.

Expected Deliverables:

Payload train design document

Anti-spin hardware

Post-flight imu stability analysis

Power System for Extreme Cold

Summary of the Project

Batteries are chemical devices, and chemistry nearly stops at stratospheric temperatures — a battery that delivers full capacity on the bench can die in minutes at −40 °C. Scope of the Work:

Design the power subsystem: energy budget for 3+ hour mission, battery chemistry trade study (Li-primary vs Li-ion at −40 °C), regulators, power distribution, current monitoring, and cold-chamber (dry ice) endurance testing.

Title of the Project

Telemetry Radio Link (LoRa / APRS)

Area of Work / Key Skills & Tools

RF basics, LoRa/packet radio, antenna fundamentals, link budget math

Difficulty | Recommended Team Size

Advanced | 3 students

Summary of the Project

The radio link is the mission's lifeline — 30+ km of slant range on milliwatts of power, from a transmitter dangling under a balloon to your ground station. LoRa modulation makes this possible for students: its spread-spectrum design decodes signals below the noise floor, so a 25 mW 433 MHz transmitter can reach hundreds of kilometres line-of-sight. Your challenges are the airborne antenna (a quarter-wave wire must be tuned and polarized correctly while swinging), the link budget, and regulatory limits on power and duty cycle. Ground range testing is deceptive — a few km on the ground with obstacles roughly equals hundreds airborne with clear line-of-sight, and you must quantify that. Start with the Friis transmission equation and a pair of LoRa modules on the bench.

Scope of the Work:

Build the airborne transmitter: LoRa 433 MHz (and/or APRS if amateur licensed) sending GPS + sensor packets. Design the airborne antenna, perform range tests (ground-to-ground several km), and characterize the real link budget.

Expected Deliverables:

Flight transmitter module + airborne antenna

Range test report

Packet format spec (with bsat-sws-2026-004)

Title of the Project

Flight Termination / Cut-Down Mechanism

Area of Work / Key Skills & Tools

Actuator design, embedded control, FMEA, redundancy design

Difficulty | Recommended Team Size

Advanced | 3 students

Summary of the Project

A cut-down device separates the payload from the balloon on command — the mission's emergency brake if the flight drifts toward an airport or the border.
